NCCN EW-1 · Workup & Initial Assessment
Step 1 · NCCN EW-1
Multidisciplinary Evaluation & Biopsy
All patients must be evaluated by an MDT at a sarcoma specialty center
▾
⚠ Biopsy incision placement MUST involve the orthopedic oncologist who will perform definitive surgery. Limb salvage depends on correct biopsy track. Primary resection at initial diagnosis is almost never appropriate.
MDT Core Team
  • Orthopedic oncologist
  • Bone pathologist
  • Medical/pediatric oncologist
  • Radiation oncologist
  • Musculoskeletal radiologist
Biopsy Principles (BONE-A)
  • Perform at treating institution
  • Biopsy from soft tissue component preferred (avoids fracture risk)
  • Reserve undecalcified bone for molecular studies — strong acid decalcification denatures DNA/RNA
  • Fresh tissue for cytogenetics & NGS preferred
  • Core needle biopsy acceptable if adequate tissue obtained
Step 2 · NCCN EW-1
Imaging Workup
Obtain ALL studies before starting chemotherapy
▾
Required Imaging
  • Contrast-enhanced MRI of primary site (mandatory)
  • CT of primary site
  • Chest CT (with or without contrast) — low-dose non-contrast for restaging
  • FDG-PET/CT (preferred) head-to-toe — can replace bone scan
  • Bone scan if FDG-PET/CT not available
Bone Marrow & Staging
  • Consider BM biopsy ± aspiration — may be omitted if FDG-PET/CT used in localized disease with no bone metastases (sensitivity 100%, specificity 96%)
  • Screening MRI spine/pelvis if clinically indicated
  • Skip metastasis evaluation for appendicular tumors — image entire involved bone (15.8% incidence)
  • Whole-body MRI may provide additional staging information
ℹ FDG-PET/CT and bone scan have high correlation for Ewing sarcoma. BM metastasis as sole site: only 1.2% of patients; 4.8% overall in newly diagnosed disease (systematic review, N=141).
Step 3 · NCCN EW-1
Molecular Workup & Labs
Required before initiation of treatment
▾
Molecular / Biomarker Testing
  • FISH EWSR1 break-apart probe — 1st line (≥90% cases)
  • RT-PCR for EWSR1::FLI1 / EWSR1::ERG (most common fusions)
  • Cytogenetics / conventional karyotype
  • If above negative → MGPT / NGS fusion panel (NCCN category 2A) to detect FUS fusions or CIC/BCOR rearrangements
  • Note: FUS fusions = EWSR1 FISH negative → require NGS
Labs & Other
  • LDH — adverse prognostic if elevated
  • CBC, CMP with calcium
  • Fertility consultation must be offered (NCCN 2026 revision)
  • Cardiac function (echo/MUGA) prior to anthracyclines
  • Renal function (Cr, GFR) prior to ifosfamide
Staging System (NCCN)
  • Ewing sarcoma is staged as Localized or Metastatic (no standard AJCC staging system routinely used)
  • Localized: no spread beyond primary site or regional nodes (CT/MRI/PET-negative)
  • Metastatic: any confirmed distant site — lung, bone, BM, soft tissue
  • Regional LN involvement → pathological confirmation required
Step 4 · Prognostic Assessment
Risk Stratification
NCI PDQ / COG pre-treatment factors
▾
Adverse Prognostic Factors
  • Metastatic disease — strongest predictor
  • Axial/pelvic primary (vs distal extremity)
  • Tumor volume >100–200 mL or >8 cm
  • Age >10–18 years (RR 1.4 vs <10y; RR 2.5 vs adults)
  • Male sex
  • Elevated LDH
  • Pathological fracture (controversial)
  • Detectable ctDNA at diagnosis (3y EFS 48.6% vs 82.1%)
  • STAG2 loss by IHC (EFS 54% vs 75%)
  • Gain 1q / loss 16q (der(16)t(1;16))
  • Complex karyotype (>5 abnormalities)
Favorable Factors
  • Localized disease
  • Distal extremity primary
  • Small tumor volume (<100 mL)
  • Age <10 years
  • Female sex
  • Extraskeletal primary (HR EFS 0.62 vs bone)
  • Good histologic response: <10% viable tumor
  • Pulmonary-only metastases (vs bone/BM)
  • Unilateral lung involvement (vs bilateral)
  • Complete clearance of lung nodules on induction (OS 71.2% vs 50.2%)
Systemic Therapy · BONE-B · COG Standard Regimens
Induction · NCCN EW-1
Interval-Compressed VDC/IE
Category 1 — COG AEWS0031 standard · At least 9 weeks prior to local therapy
▾
✓ AEWS0031 (JCO 2012, 10y follow-up JCO 2023): Interval-compressed VDC/IE every 2 weeks → 10y EFS 70% vs 61% (q3w), 10y OS 76% vs 69% (P=.04). No additional toxicity.

Cycle Schedule
  • Alternating VDC → IE → VDC → IE every 14 days (interval-compressed)
  • NCCN: category 1 evidence for 28–49 weeks total depending on regimen/risk
  • Longer pre-local-control therapy may be considered for metastatic disease based on response
  • Recovery: ANC ≥0.75×10⁹/L, platelets ≥75×10⁹/L typically required
  • Note: actinomycin-D (dactinomycin) may be used in consolidation but NOT during RT
ℹ EE2012 (Lancet 2022): VDC/IE vs VIDE — HR EFS 0.71, HR OS 0.62, posterior probability >99% in favor of VDC/IE. Less febrile neutropenia. VDC/IE now internationally adopted over VIDE.
Consolidation · Localized Disease
Post-Local Control Chemotherapy
Continue VDC/IE based on local control outcome · Category 1
▾
Negative / Close Margins (R0/R1 equivalent)
  • Continue chemotherapy (cat.1) ± consider RT depending on margin status
  • Total chemotherapy duration: 28–49 weeks depending on schedule used
  • IE/VC ×5 additional cycles for standard-risk
Positive Margins (R2 equivalent)
  • RT + chemotherapy (category 1 for chemotherapy)
  • RT concurrently with consolidation chemotherapy
  • Postoperative RT should begin within 60 days of surgery
⚠ AEWS1031 (JCO 2021): Addition of VTC (vincristine-topotecan-cyclophosphamide) every 3rd cycle did NOT improve outcomes. 5y EFS 78% vs 79% (ns). VDC/IE remains the standard backbone — no improvement shown by adding VTC.
High-Dose Chemotherapy + ASCT — US Position
  • Not standard per COG/NCCN for localized disease treated with intensive VDC/IE
  • EE99 R2loc showed BuMel benefit over VIDE consolidation, but VIDE is less intensive than VDC/IE — benefit not extrapolated
  • COG: BuMel advantage in VIDE-treated population cannot justify ASCT after VDC/IE
  • High-dose therapy: select patients at high relapse risk — no level A1 evidence for COG regimens

Local Control & Radiation Therapy · NCCN EW-2 · BONE-C
Restaging · After ≥9 Weeks Chemotherapy
Restaging Before Local Control Decision
Use same imaging technique as initial workup
▾
  • Chest CT (low-dose non-contrast preferred)
  • Contrast-enhanced MRI + CT (without contrast) of primary site
  • X-rays of primary site
  • Consider FDG-PET/CT (head-to-toe) or bone scan — correlates with histologic response
  • Decreased PET uptake correlates with good histologic response and better outcome
  • Repeat other abnormal baseline studies
Surgery · NCCN EW-2 · BONE-A
Surgical Local Control
Preferred approach for resectable lesions — wide excision with clear margins
▾
✓ Surgery is generally preferred if the lesion is resectable with acceptable functional/cosmetic outcome. The superiority of surgery over RT has never been tested in a prospective RCT — apparent superiority may reflect selection bias.
Indications for Surgery
  • Resectable lesion with anticipated adequate margins
  • Acceptable functional and cosmetic outcome predicted
  • Limb salvage preferred over amputation when feasible
  • Wide excision target: histologically negative margins
  • Amputation in selected cases (NCCN EW-2)
Outcomes by Margin Status
  • Negative margins: 8y local failure 5%, OS 94% (St. Jude)
  • Positive margins: 8y local failure 17%, OS 71%
  • Histologic response (<10% viable): strong predictor of EFS
  • Complete necrosis → particularly favorable regardless of margin
  • Necrotic tumor at margin = considered negative margin if complete pathologic response (NCCN BONE-D 2026)
⚠ Timing: Local therapy initiated 6–15 weeks → 5y OS 78.7%; >16 weeks → 70.4% (P<.001). Difference more pronounced for RT-alone patients (NCDB, N=1,318).
Pelvic Ewing — Special Considerations
  • Pelvic tumors: COG INT-0091 → no difference in local control/survival by modality (surgery alone vs RT alone vs combined)
  • EE99 pelvic data: combined RT+surgery associated with improved OS vs surgery alone, especially non-sacral (OS 72% vs 47%, P=.024)
  • NCCN: chemotherapy (cat.1) + consider RT for pelvic tumors
  • Adverse factors for pelvic local failure: large volume, periacetabular site, RT-only approach
Radiation Therapy · BONE-C
RT Doses & Indications
Delivered with stringent planning techniques by experienced Ewing sarcoma RT teams
▾
Definitive RT
55.8 Gy / 1.8 Gy/fr
  • Pre-chemo volume + 2 cm margin
  • Initial 45 Gy to pre-chemo tumor
  • Boost +10.8 Gy to post-chemo volume
  • No difference vs RT to entire bone (RCT)
Post-op Microscopic (R1)
50.4 Gy / 1.8 Gy/fr
  • Also: 45 Gy + 5.4 Gy boost
  • Begin within 60 days of surgery
  • Given concurrently with consolidation chemo
Gross Residual (R2)
45+10.8 Gy = 55.8 Gy
  • 45 Gy to original disease site
  • +10.8 Gy boost for gross residual
  • (INT-0091 / St. Jude protocol)
Dose Escalation — Laskar 2022 (RCT)
  • Unresectable Ewing sarcoma (N=95)
  • 70.2 Gy vs 55.8 Gy / 1.8 Gy/fr
  • 5y local control: 76.4% vs 49.4% (P=.02)
  • 5y DFS: 46.7% vs 31.8% (ns, P=.22)
  • 5y OS: 58.8% vs 45.4% (ns, P=.08)
  • Skin toxicity G>2: 10.4% vs 2.1%
  • Improved local control but no survival benefit — likely due to unchanged metastatic rate
High-Dose Escalation — Single-Institution
  • Tumors ≥8 cm → 64.8 Gy median dose (Kacar 2024, Cancer)
  • 5y cumulative incidence of local failure: 6.6%
  • Favorable comparison to published local failure rates
  • Higher local failure in: age >14y + tumor >8 cm
Preoperative RT
  • May be considered for marginally resectable tumors
  • Given concurrently with consolidation chemotherapy (BONE-C 2026)
  • Delphi consensus: strong agreement — preop RT when inadequate margin foreseen
  • Goal: achieve clear margins at surgery
Second Malignancy Risk
  • ≥60 Gy: secondary sarcoma incidence 20%
  • 48–60 Gy: secondary sarcoma 5%
  • <48 Gy: secondary sarcoma 0%
  • RT + anthracyclines + alkylants: OR ×2 for secondary sarcoma (PENTEC)
RT Technique (BONE-C)
  • IMRT/VMAT: standard technique, dose conformality reduces late effects
  • Proton therapy: treatment plans show superior normal tissue sparing vs IMRT (Rombi 2012) — no difference in local control or functional outcome established yet; consider for pediatric/central axis cases
  • SBRT: for metastatic bone/soft tissue sites (see metastatic section)
  • Hyperfractionated RT: NOT associated with improved local control or reduced morbidity
  • RT is delivered in setting with stringent planning by experienced Ewing sarcoma teams
RT Target Volumes
Target Volume Delineation
NCI PDQ / COG approach
▾
Definitive RT Volumes
  • Initial phase: 45 Gy to pre-chemotherapy tumor volume
  • Margin: 2 cm around pre-chemo GTV (anatomical barriers respected)
  • Boost phase: +10.8 Gy to post-chemotherapy volume
  • Boost restricted to residual mass with smaller margin
  • Alternative: 55.8 Gy to pre-chemo volume + 2 cm (no difference in RCT)
Post-operative RT Volumes
  • Clinical target includes surgical bed + scar/drain sites in phase 1
  • Phase 1 (45 Gy): operative bed
  • Phase 2 boost: areas of residual disease / highest risk
  • Chest wall with pleural effusion/invasion → consider hemithorax RT
Special Sites
  • Chest wall: Pleural invasion / effusion / intraoperative contamination → hemithorax RT. EFS trend better with hemithorax RT (not significant). Vertebral tumors: lower EFS probability without hemithorax RT
  • Spine/sacrum: RT required due to surgical complexity; dose limited by cord tolerance
  • Pelvic: combined RT+surgery favored over surgery alone (EE99 data)
Metastatic Disease · NCCN EW-3 · NCI PDQ
Epidemiology & Prognosis
Metastatic Ewing Sarcoma — Overview
~25% of patients at diagnosis · Poor overall prognosis
▾
All metastatic: 6y EFS ~28%, OS ~30%
Lung only: 6y EFS ~40% (with WLI)
Bone only: 4y EFS ~28%
Lung + bone/BM: 4y EFS ~14%
Independent Poor Prognostic Factors (Metastatic)
  • Age >14 years
  • Primary tumor volume >200 mL
  • More than one bone metastatic site
  • Bone marrow involvement
  • Additional pulmonary metastases (combined sites)
  • Bilateral lung involvement vs unilateral
  • Lack of complete pulmonary response to induction (OS 50.2% vs 71.2%)
Treatment Algorithm · NCCN EW-3
Metastatic Treatment Strategy
Stratified by extent of metastatic disease
▾
🫁
Lung only — Partial Response to induction: Resection + Whole Lung Irradiation (WLI)
🫁
Lung only — Complete Response to induction: Consider WLI (NCCN EW-3)
🎯
Oligometastatic: Local control to primary site + Excision of all metastatic sites if possible
⚠
Widely metastatic (cannot deliver local control to all sites): Continuing chemotherapy ± palliative surgery; Palliative RT to symptomatic areas; Other ablative techniques
⚠ Local control of all metastatic sites: Retrospective data (N=120 PDMES) — RT to primary + all metastases → 3y EFS 39% vs 17%/14% for partial or no local treatment (P<.001). Caution: significant selection bias.
Whole Lung Irradiation (WLI)
WLI Doses & Indications
For all patients with pulmonary metastases — even if CR on chemotherapy
▾
WLI Dose (NCI PDQ / NCCN)
12–15 Gy bilateral lungs
  • Doses modulated based on: age, lung volume irradiated, pulmonary function
  • No prospective RCT proving OS benefit in modern era
  • Recommended even for complete responders (NCCN "consider WLI")
  • Early 1970s intergroup trial: WLI improved OS with VAC vs VAC alone
Rapid pulmonary CR (Reiter 2024)
  • 41.2% of metastatic pulmonary patients achieved radiographic CR on induction
  • CR group: 5y OS 71.2% vs 50.2% residual nodules
  • Early CR + consolidative WLI → 5y OS 85.2%
  • Argues for WLI even in CR group
Metastatic RT — Bone & Soft Tissue
SBRT for Metastatic Sites
NCI PDQ — Brown 2014
▾
SBRT — Curative/Definitive Intent
40 Gy / 5 fr median (range 30–60 Gy / 3–10 fr)
  • Bone and soft tissue metastatic sites
  • Biologically equivalent to standard fractionation doses
Palliative SBRT
40 Gy / 5 fr median (range 16–50 Gy / 1–10 fr)
  • Short-course (<10 fractions): 85% complete/partial pain relief in pediatric cohorts
⚠ High-dose chemotherapy + HSCT for metastatic Ewing sarcoma (bone/BM): NOT shown to improve EFS over standard chemotherapy. No graft-versus-tumor effect demonstrated. Not standard per NCI PDQ/COG for de novo metastatic disease.
Surveillance · NCCN EW-2 · NCI PDQ
Follow-up Schedule
NCCN EW-2 Surveillance Protocol
Indefinite surveillance — category 2B after 5 years (NCCN 2026)
▾
0–24 months
  • Physical exam
  • Contrast-enhanced MRI of primary site
  • CT of primary site
  • Chest imaging (x-ray or CT) every 3 months
  • X-rays of primary site
  • CBC and labs as indicated
24 months → 5 years
  • Increase intervals for physical exam and imaging
  • Chest and primary imaging — less frequent
  • Consider FDG-PET/CT or bone scan as clinically indicated
> 5 years
  • Annual follow-up (indefinitely)
  • Category 2B
  • Late relapse: 13% occur >5y from diagnosis (higher than other pediatric solid tumors)
  • Black race, male, older age, pelvic/axial primary → higher late death risk
ℹ Late relapse is a major concern: SEER analysis — of 1,351 patients surviving >60 months, 209 died; 144 (69%) from recurrent Ewing sarcoma. Late deaths associated with pelvic/axial primary, older age, male, Black race.
Late Effects Monitoring
Long-Term Toxicity Surveillance
Organ-specific monitoring for treatment-related late effects
▾
Cardiotoxicity (Anthracyclines)
  • Doxorubicin cumulative dose ≤375 mg/m² in most COG regimens
  • Cardiac surveillance: echo every 1–5 years depending on total dose and RT field
  • PENTEC: Dmean 20 Gy → IC 1.1%, coronary 2.1%, any cardiac event 3.8%
  • +100 mg/m² anthracycline ≈ +10.5 Gy equivalent cardiac risk
Nephrotoxicity (Ifosfamide)
  • Ifosfamide cumulative: 9 g/m²/cycle × multiple cycles
  • Renal function annually (GFR, tubular function)
  • PENTEC: V10 >40% → grade 2 renal failure 10% with ifosfamide
  • Fanconi syndrome: tubulopathy, electrolyte monitoring
Pulmonary (WLI / Thoracic RT)
  • PFTs annually if WLI received
  • V20 <30% or Dmean <12 Gy → <5% grade 2 pneumonitis
  • WLI 12–15 Gy: late pulmonary fibrosis risk — long-term PFT monitoring
Secondary Malignancy
  • Lifelong surveillance — secondary sarcomas peak at 5–10y
  • RT >60 Gy: 20% secondary sarcoma risk
  • RT combined with alkylants + anthracyclines: OR ×2
  • Annual clinical exam of prior RT field
Relapse / Refractory Disease · NCI PDQ · rEECur
Prognosis at Relapse
Epidemiology & Prognostic Factors
5y OS after relapse: 10–15% · No standardized 2nd-line treatment
▾
Overall 5y OS: 10–15%
Late relapse (>2y): 5y OS 30%
Early relapse (<2y): 5y OS 7%
80% relapse within 2 years
  • Time to recurrence: most important prognostic factor at relapse (>2y → 5y OS 30% vs 7%)
  • Local + distant: worse than isolated local or isolated metastatic relapse
  • 6-month EFS rate at relapse: only 13% (COG cooperative group phase II data)
  • Italian/Scandinavian: younger age + longer DFI + lung-only → better PFS after relapse
  • Prior ASCT → less likely to achieve 2nd CR
rEECur Trial — Reference Standard
International Randomized Trial for Relapsed/Refractory ES
MAMS phase II/III · >650 patients · High-dose ifosfamide = current reference arm
▾
⚠ rEECur is the first RCT comparing second-line regimens for relapsed/refractory Ewing sarcoma. High-dose ifosfamide emerged as most effective despite significant renal and neurological toxicity.
Arm Hierarchy (closed in order)
  • GD (gem-docetaxel) — closed 1st (least effective)
  • IT (irinotecan-temozolomide) — closed 2nd
  • TC (topotecan-cyclophosphamide) — closed 3rd; IFOS > TC in phase III
  • CE (carboplatin-etoposide) — closed August 2024
  • HD-IFOS (15 g/m², 5d CI q21d) — current reference arm
  • IFOS-L (IFOS + lenvatinib) — ongoing since June 2023
HD-IFOS Results
  • Ifosfamide: 15 g/m² continuous infusion × 5 days
  • Cycle: every 21 days
  • Median PFS: 5.1 months
  • Median OS: 14.4 months
  • Toxicities G3+: febrile neutropenia 30%, encephalopathy 5%, renal 2%

RT at Relapse · NCCN EW-2
Local Control Strategies at Relapse
▾
  • Surgery: aggressive resection (including amputation/hemipelvectomy) may be considered for non-metastatic local relapse, even with limited prognosis
  • Pulmonary metastasectomy: controversial for isolated lung relapse — no clear benefit shown
  • WLI at relapse: patients with lung relapse who have not received prior WLI — consider WLI ± SBRT; residual disease may be surgically removed
  • SBRT: for oligometastatic bone/soft tissue relapse sites
  • Palliative RT: short course (<10 fr) — 85% pain relief in pediatric cohorts
  • Bone RT: may provide palliation; radical resection may further improve outcome

Points of Convergence (2022–2026)
  • Both use VDC/IE q14d as standard backbone (EE2012 result)
  • Both endorse multidisciplinary expert center evaluation
  • Both mandate biopsy at treating institution
  • Both use FDG-PET/CT for staging
  • Both endorse proton therapy for select pediatric/central cases
  • Both use rEECur HD-IFOS as preferred 2nd-line reference
  • Both require RT within 60 days of surgery
  • Both endorse SBRT for metastatic bone/soft tissue
Key Remaining Differences
  • RT dose: US 55.8 Gy vs EU 54 Gy (∆1.8 Gy — 1 fraction)
  • WLI dose: US 12–15 Gy (age-independent) vs EU 15/18 Gy by age
  • ASCT for localized high-risk: EU uses BuMel (EE99 R2loc); US does NOT extrapolate (VIDE context)
  • Risk stratification: EU explicitly stratifies by volume+histo response for BuMel; US primarily localized vs metastatic
  • Dose escalation evidence: US has RCT data (70.2 Gy Laskar 2022) for unresectable; EU mainly institutional (64.8 Gy)
  • Surveillance: US guidelines specify indefinitely annual (cat.2B) — more explicit late follow-up recommendation
