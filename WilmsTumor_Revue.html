Clinical Signal 2025–2026

The SJWT21 trial at St. Jude is the most significant RT innovation in Wilms tumor in years: proton beam with a conformal reduced retroperitoneal target for stage III–V, aiming to eliminate scoliosis, organ growth asymmetry and secondary malignancies. The first 13 enrolled patients have been described dosimetrically, with bowel gas now the key technical challenge for proton planning in the abdomen.

The new COG AREN2231 study represents a fundamental shift in risk stratification: 1q gain (~30% of FHWT) now enters clinical decision-making alongside classic LOH 1p/16q and blastemal predominance. This may reclassify a significant portion of currently "standard risk" patients into higher-risk treatment arms. Liquid biopsy (peripheral blood) will be explored as a 1q gain detection method.

Clinical Signal 2025–2026

The WLI omission strategy in complete responders is increasingly adopted on both sides of the Atlantic, but the prospective evidence base is thin. AREN2231 will formally study this in a standardized way for the first time in COG. The key unresolved issue is how to define CR — CXR alone vs CT, and the threshold for treating CT-only nodules.

The major technical advance is cardiac-sparing WL IMRT combined with dosimetrically matched abdominal fields. Sequential planning significantly raises cardiac and pulmonary doses — in upcoming COG protocols, flank RT will be deliberately delayed to week 6 to allow concurrent delivery and avoid high-dose field overlap at the field junction. IROC pre-review of IMRT plans is now mandatory for COG WT protocols.

Clinical Signal 2025–2026

Diffuse anaplasia remains the most challenging subtype, with OS after relapse under 50% despite intensive therapy. The AREN1921 trial studying UH-3 (6-drug regimen) is the current pivot trial — it aims to improve outcomes for newly diagnosed DAWT and high/very-high risk relapsed FHWT by intensifying chemotherapy beyond the previous UH-1/UH-2 regimens.

RT dose escalation (19.8 Gy stage III diffuse vs 10.8 Gy FH) is established but local control rates for DAWT stage III remain ~95% — systemic failure is the dominant pattern. The addition of irinotecan to the regimen for stage IV DAWT reflects the emerging role of topoisomerase inhibitors in Wilms tumor high-risk biology.

Clinical Signal 2025–2026

Bilateral Wilms tumor remains the most complex surgical and oncological challenge, with 8-year EFS of 75% from AREN0534 — lagging behind unilateral disease. The long-term follow-up published in 2024 shows that relapse risk is front-loaded: the hazard is highest immediately after diagnosis and declines over 2 years, which has implications for surveillance intensity scheduling.

The proton SJWT21 trial offers a particularly important innovation for bilateral disease: it tests whether partial kidney proton beam RT to involved surgical margins can achieve equivalent local control to whole kidney fields — potentially preserving functional renal parenchyma and reducing the risk of chronic renal insufficiency in this vulnerable population where bilateral nephrectomy is already a risk.

Clinical Signal 2025–2026

The relapse landscape is being restructured by the AREN1921 trial, which introduces a systematic 3-tier risk stratification for relapsed FHWT and standardizes salvage regimens. The critical unmet need is in very-high-risk relapse (2nd relapse or post-intensive therapy) where OS historically falls below 30%.

The most promising biology-driven angle is WNT pathway targeting via tegavivint — a subset of Wilms tumors carry activating β-catenin mutations and may respond selectively. At the same time, 1q gain as a liquid biopsy marker could enable early detection of relapse and real-time risk reclassification without requiring a new biopsy — a genuine paradigm shift if validated in AREN2231.
